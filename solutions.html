Technical approaches

Cooling systems can be redesigned to reduce water dependence.

Closed-loop systems

  • Recirculate coolant instead of depending on continuous evaporation.
  • Reduce ongoing community freshwater demand.
  • Offer a stronger long-term sustainability baseline.

Direct-to-chip and immersion cooling

  • Bring liquid cooling closer to the heat source.
  • Improve heat transfer for dense AI hardware.
  • Can reduce overall cooling energy when deployed well.

How they work

See each solution as a process, not just a slogan.

Reclaimed water

Treated wastewater > Cooling system > Less pressure on drinking water

Instead of drawing fresh potable water first, facilities can use treated reclaimed water for cooling where infrastructure allows it.

Smarter siting

Cooler climate > Lower cooling burden > Less evaporation risk

Location shapes water demand before a server is even switched on. Cooler and less-stressed regions reduce the system burden from day one.

Waste heat reuse

Server heat > Capture loop > Homes, greenhouses, industry

Instead of throwing away excess heat, a capture system redirects it into nearby uses so the facility returns local value instead of only consuming resources.

Operational approaches

Infrastructure decisions outside the server rack matter too.

01

Use reclaimed water

Cooling systems do not always need potable water. Recycled and reclaimed water from treated wastewater streams can reduce pressure on drinking water supplies, especially in regions where communities are already competing for limited freshwater. This shifts the question from "how much water is used?" to "what kind of water is being used, and who needs it most?"

02

Choose sites carefully

Cooler climates and less stressed watersheds can reduce the burden of water-intensive thermal management. Where a data center is built affects how hard cooling systems have to work, how much water may evaporate, and whether the facility adds strain to an already vulnerable local ecosystem. Siting is not just a business decision. It is a water justice decision.

03

Reuse waste heat

Data centers produce enormous amounts of heat, and too often that heat is simply expelled into the air as a byproduct. Waste heat reuse treats that heat as a resource instead of a loss. In some places, captured data center heat can be redirected into district heating networks, nearby buildings, greenhouses, or industrial processes. This does not eliminate the water footprint on its own, but it improves the overall efficiency of the system and helps communities see data centers as infrastructure that should return value locally, not just extract resources quietly.
